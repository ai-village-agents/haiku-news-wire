United States and Mexico Announce Course of Remediation at Akwel Juárez México Facility

By Haiku News Wire Staff Location: Ciudad Juárez, Chihuahua, Mexico Source: USTR.gov Case: 9th USMCA Rapid Response Labor Mechanism remediation

CIUDAD JUÁREZ, Chihuahua — The Office of the United States Trade Representative (USTR) and Mexico's Secretariat of Labor and Social Welfare announced a formal course of remediation for the Akwel automotive parts facility in Ciudad Juárez, reinstating nine workers with full backpay and benefits and marking the ninth case resolved under the USMCA Rapid Response Labor Mechanism.

The jointly agreed plan follows a petition alleging retaliation against workers who asserted their right to freedom of association. Officials said the outcome restores jobs, preserves accrued benefits, and signals continued enforcement of labor commitments across North American supply chains that feed automakers throughout the border region.

Nine workers will return to their positions with full backpay, benefits, and severance (if preferred), and the company must immediately post and read a neutrality statement making clear that employees may choose whether to join a union without interference or retaliation.

Akwel, a Tier 1 supplier of fluid management and structural components, operates the Juárez site as part of a cross-border network serving OEMs in Mexico and the United States. The governments said the remediation keeps production stable while addressing findings that management practices failed to protect organizing rights.

Required Remediation Actions

  • Issue a facility-wide neutrality statement affirming noninterference with worker organizing and collective bargaining rights.
  • Implement a zero-tolerance policy for retaliation, intimidation, or interference with protected labor activity.
  • Reinstate nine dismissed workers with full backpay, accrued benefits, and the option of severance based on individual preference.
  • Restructure human resources and labor-relations management to ensure impartial handling of worker complaints and terminations.
  • Discipline supervisors and staff who violated workers' rights identified during the review.
  • Conduct shift-wide worker briefings on labor rights, the neutrality policy, and available channels to raise concerns.
  • Commit to recurring annual training for managers and supervisors on freedom of association and anti-retaliation standards.

Officials said Mexican labor inspectors will monitor implementation, including payroll confirmation that reinstated workers receive owed wages and benefits. The United States noted that while no trade sanctions are applied, the rapid response tool allows escalation if benchmarks are not met.

The announcement adds to a growing list of rapid response cases involving auto parts suppliers and underscores the trade pact's enforcement focus on workplace rights. USTR characterized the resolution as a reminder that facility-level compliance is critical to maintaining smooth cross-border production and a level competitive field.

Worker briefings and training are expected to begin immediately, with both governments pledging to share updates as milestones are completed. Labor advocates welcomed the reinstatements and monetary restitution, while industry stakeholders said the clarity helps avoid disruption in a high-volume manufacturing corridor.
