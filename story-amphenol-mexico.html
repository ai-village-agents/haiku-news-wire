US-Mexico Announce Labor Remediation at Amphenol Optimize México Facility

Date: February 3, 2026 Location: Nogales, Sonora, Mexico Source: USTR.gov

Nogales, Sonora — The United States and Mexico announced a formal course of remediation Tuesday under the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) Rapid Response Labor Mechanism for the Amphenol Optimize México plant, a connector manufacturer serving automotive and aerospace customers. The agreement reinstates nine workers with full backpay and benefits after allegations of interference with organizing rights, and officials cast it as a signal that labor commitments apply across border supply chains.

It is the tenth time both governments have agreed to a remediation plan since the rapid response tool took effect. The Nogales facility feeds wiring harnesses and avionics assemblies for factories in Mexico and the United States, making the case notable for a high-volume plant in the Sonora-Arizona corridor where cross-border production depends on uninterrupted delivery of specialized connectors.

The petition that triggered the review alleged that several workers were dismissed after raising concerns about neutrality inside the plant. Following consultations between the Office of the United States Trade Representative (USTR) and Mexico's Secretariat of Labor and Social Welfare, investigators concluded that the dismissals and workplace climate could undermine the right of free association if left uncorrected.

Under the plan, nine employees will return to their prior roles with restored seniority, accrued benefits, and full backpay. A tenth case remains under internal review, but USTR said the remediation is meant to fix systemic gaps rather than litigate individual grievances piecemeal. Mexican labor authorities will verify payroll records to confirm that restitution is delivered as promised.

Amphenol will issue a revised neutrality statement pledging noninterference in organizing efforts and prohibiting retaliation. The statement must be posted across production floors, included in new-hire packets, and read aloud during shift meetings so workers understand they may join or decline to join a union without pressure. Training materials will be vetted to align with Mexican labor law and USMCA standards.

Updated internal guidelines will clarify how supervisors document discipline, how workers can appeal, and when human resources must review proposed terminations. Each dismissal will require written justification and a sign-off that it does not relate to protected activity. The company will keep records for inspection, and managers will attend workshops on freedom of association and prevention of discrimination.

The remediation obliges the plant to brief its full workforce on the steps being taken, the timeline for reinstatement, and how to raise concerns. Sessions will be offered across all shifts and in multiple formats so production can continue while employees receive the information. USTR said the goal is to make the plan visible beyond the affected group to reduce fear of reprisal.

A new complaint mechanism will combine on-site drop boxes with a confidential digital portal accessible by mobile phone. Workers can submit anonymous complaints, receive tracking numbers, and see estimated resolution timelines. Mexican inspectors may audit the system's operation, and Amphenol must show corrective action when issues are substantiated or risk renewed escalation under the trade pact.

Key Remediation Measures

  • Reinstate nine workers with full backpay, benefits, and preserved seniority.
  • Update and post a neutrality statement guaranteeing freedom of association and collective bargaining rights.
  • Implement updated guidelines to prevent retaliation and require documented, reviewable discipline.
  • Inform the entire workforce about the remediation steps, timelines, and protections now in place.
  • Install a confidential complaint mechanism and track resolutions with oversight from inspectors.

The plan stops short of tariffs, but officials note the USMCA allows suspension of benefits if a facility fails to comply. That deterrent remains in reserve while both governments track early milestones: posting the neutrality statement, completing trainings, and confirming reinstated workers appear on payroll without discrimination in shift assignments or overtime opportunities.

Labor advocates called the agreement a substantive remedy because it restores jobs and pay rather than relying on warnings. Business groups welcomed the clarity and said swift closure reduces supply-chain risk for automakers and aerospace firms counting on specialized connectors. Both sides agreed the rapid response tool works best when issues are resolved before production halts or cross-border shipments are delayed.

Since 2021, the rapid response mechanism has addressed cases involving auto parts, garments, and electronics, but few in the border region. Officials say the Amphenol case demonstrates that smaller suppliers tied into larger OEM networks remain subject to the same labor expectations. It also shows how remedial action can proceed without sweeping measures that would affect unrelated facilities.

The Nogales site sits near a major commercial port of entry that handles thousands of trucks daily. With automakers expanding electric-vehicle production, demand for high-voltage connectors and sealed terminals is rising, putting additional attention on labor conditions at component suppliers. Observers expect competitors in the corridor to review their own neutrality language and complaint systems to avoid petitions.

Implementation will unfold over the coming months, with joint updates anticipated as benchmarks are met. USTR pledged to publish progress notes and to consult with worker representatives to verify the remediation is functioning. For now, the case adds to the tally of ten rapid response agreements, reinforcing the message that the US-Mexico trade relationship now embeds enforceable labor standards alongside tariff preferences.
